Maya Universe Academy

Spandan Marasini / Jan 20, 2020

Nestled on the hills of a small highway town in central Nepal, Maya Universe Academy occupies a unique space within the development of education in the country. It is proudly labeled as Nepal’s first and only free-private school, flowering in a poor nation where high-quality education does not come cheap.

In May of 2019, the Kathmandu Post reported that even though public education received 10.68% of the annual budget, that was only half of what had been promised by the government. This might be the most recent example of how the nation has stalled in developing its public education system, and further numbers are there to back it up: in the country’s most academically advanced city, Kathmandu, over 70% of primary and secondary school students attend private schools. However, in less developed parts of the country, with an average GDP per capita of just $835, financial capital for households to fund private education is not available. Therefore, many families just cannot afford high-quality private education and are forced to send their children to underfunded and understaffed public schools.

Enter Manjil Rana

Seeing a major gap in the access to high-quality education for children all around the country, Manjil Rana and a cohort of youths created the first Maya Universe Academy in Udindhunga, Tanahun. Now, the group has gone on to establish a total of three schools, educating over 400 children in some of Nepal’s most rural communities.

Rana said the following in a 2018 presentation in Kathmandu, summarily capturing the uniqueness of his social enterprise.

We’re not a charity, we haven’t created an NGO here asking for donations, we have figured out a way to run an institution through entrepreneurship that makes sure that every child studies for free, and we raise enough money to run the school in the process

In some of the most economically barren areas of the country, he and his team have managed to create private institutions that have become self-sustainable.

Maya’s Inception

When Manjil Rana tells the story of Maya, he tells it from a perspective of a student who ended up in the United States for his higher education, but was disillusioned after hearing stories of Nepalis going abroad to work, which heavily influenced his decision to return. During the same 2018 presentation, Rana said, “You might have a master’s degree, but you might be in that [foreign] country washing dishes. It’s so hard to even get a visa, and when you get it you feel like something amazing has happened… we Nepalis do not have much respect for ourselves when we are abroad.” And so, in 2011, Rana dropped out of his university in the United States and decided to begin his journey of revolutionizing education at home.

The founding group of Maya arrived at the location of their first school in Udindhunga with nothing more than a tent. The humble location was where the school started, and to his surprise, Rana was reportedly approached by many families within the area. Unsatisfied with the local public school, these families were willing to entrust their children’s education on a young change-maker and his friends. Ultimately, though, Maya Universe had to ensure its self-dependency. And to do this, the schools created a model where every month, the parents of the children would spend two days working at the school. Initially, this meant that families would need to help construct the school’s buildings, but afterward, it meant that the patrons of the institution had to help run the school’s enterprises.

Maya’s Business Model

Parents in Nepal, on average, are paying up to rs. 500 to rs. 1,000 ($5 - $10) per month on their children’s education. These are meager sums, and many public schools are therefore reliant on dispersed NGO funding. At Maya, they figured out early that in order to run their school, they needed to find a way to raise money in a more efficient manner. Utilizing the same model as constructing the school, the founders decided that parents would now spend their two days helping make bracelets.

Manjil says, “If each family member makes 4 bracelets a day, and we sell them each for rs. 2,000 each, and after commissions for those selling the bracelets in the market, each parent could make the school around rs. 6,000 every day.” Over two days, that’s more than $100. And an additional boost to Maya is that their bracelets aren’t only being sold to Nepalis, but also to international customers via online stores. Additionally, the school has also focused on agriculture and raising animals (chicken, goats, pigs). In fact, in September 2018, an Israeli volunteer, Shoval Eidan, spent 45 days helping raise 100 anti-biotic free chicken.

Volunteer Networks

Last year, in Madrid, Spain, there was a photo exhibition about Maya dedicated to raising funds for the Maya Universe Academy. The South Asia Institute at Harvard University has a dedicated page detailing internship opportunities for Harvard students at Maya. Rejina Pariyar, a student at the Udindhunga school, spent some time in western Europe (Amsterdam and Berlin) in 2019 giving presentations at different colleges about a documentary that was made about her by Celine and Maret, two Dutch volunteers. In 2018, I also spent two months of my time at Maya Universe (Udindhunga) teaching English and giving after school classes on Mathematics, Science, and Social Studies. During my time working at Maya, I worked alongside French, Irish, and Italian volunteers and a huge cohort of over 20 students from the United States from the Reserve Officers’ Training Corps (ROTC) program.

In short, all of these examples show that Maya’s network of volunteers is vast. I went during a dry spell due to monsoon rains, which usually dissuade volunteers to make the journey. However, if one were to visit during either the spring or winter, you can expect the facility to be sprawling with volunteers. For every day spent in their facility, the institutions charge $15. This has also become a stable revenue stream for the school. Their vast volunteer network is unlike any other and is due in some parts to the closeness that volunteers experience with the students and the overall school mission.

Maya’s Social Model

Maya, in Nepali, means love, and love is a guiding principle of how all three Maya Universe Academies are run. Every morning, students, volunteers, staff, and administrators gathered in a circle. After a minute of eyes-closed self-reflection, everyone hugs the person to their left and right. Often during lunch breaks, the entire school (including the principals), can be seen playing cricket or grabbing a bite with the students. Most importantly, you would never see a child being hit by any of the teachers. In a country where corporal punishment is still heavily tolerated, and discipline is cultivated through fear rather than respect, Maya’s social model–like its economic model–is unprecedented.

Long-term results

Maya’s revolutionary economic and social models are backed up with results from the first batch of Maya students who took the Secondary Education Exam (SEE). The SEE is taken by all graduating 10th-grade students and has notoriously been known as the iron-gate in Nepali education. And the first-batch of Maya students who took the SEE exam was up for the challenge. The graduating batch of 25 students consisted of 9 B’s and 15 A’s. These are exceptional results, especially in comparison to national averages: in 2017, over half the exam-takers had a grade of C+ or lower.

The example of Maya Universe Academy provides a solid framework for running high-quality education institutions in some of the most rural parts of the country. It is also embarrassing for the public school system to be outdone by young social entrepreneurs who started from a tent. But the most important part is that these schools have set an example, that if replicated, might have the chance to change the lives of the poorest in the most underdeveloped parts of the country.
